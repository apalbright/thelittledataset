Finding an advisor (one of first things you have to do)

  • any prof with Ec PhD in boston area can be your advisor
    • sortable list of potential advisors
  • starting making appointments now
    • with a polite, short email
    • be prepped (have 2-3 questions to ask them as convo starters)
      • read end of paper with the "extensions piece" for ideas
  • sequential method of contact doesn't work

Presentations (you'll do these in Oct and Nov)

  • motivate topic/question, capture listeners interest
  • less words on a slide > more words on a slide
  • can you explain your thesis question in one figure/table?
    • practice being conceptually concise and clear
  • 15-20min presenting and 5-10min for Q&A/discussion

Thesis tools:

  • thesis is an opp to get acquianted with new tools!
  • code for data cleaning/results generation: Stata, R, Python
  • typesetting (using LaTeX): LyX, Overleaf, beamer for presentations

Suggestions wrt tools:

  • please, please always do things out of Dropbox or Drive, etc.
  • typesetting: overleaf (continuous updating; no mystery in compiling errors)
  • citations: use something! my suggestion: bibtex (google scholar gives you the bibtex format)
  • will share an example overleaf article using bibtex that you can use as template

On coding:

  • YOUR WORK SHOULD BE REPRODUCIBLE
    • there is raw data, files that clean data/generate results, and results tables/graphics
  • do-files, do-files, do-files
    • never edit excel (or other data) files directly
    • short-term gain not worth the long-term loss in lack of reproducibility and possibly misremembering the process
  • if you're interested in using 'notebooks', happy try to tell you about R notebooks, but if you're happy with Stata, continue with do-files

Programming resources:

  • Stackoverflow is your friend
  • So is Stata support through Harvard
  • So am I (more for R)

Finding data tips:

  • talk to faculty who've done related research
    • often they'll share data they have that was used in a related paper
    • sometimes they'll have a hunch on who you should contact
    • many papers have datasets available online with them
  • coldcall/email organizations/companies with a pitch and see if they'll share data with you
    • keep a log of data search attempts over time so you can keep track of what you have and haven't tried for the future
  • Ask Diane Sredl (data reference librarian) for data search help
